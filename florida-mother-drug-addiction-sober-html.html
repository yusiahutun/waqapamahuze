Florida mother details her harrowing battle with meth and opioid addiction after turning to drugs to

A mother who overcame addiction and regained custody of her children has opened up about how she abused methamphetamine and opioids to numb the pain of being sexually and physically abused as a child before she got clean. 

Sonya Johnson, 39, from Sarasota, Florida, shared that her tumultuous relationship with drugs started at the tender age of 11 when she first experimented with cigarettes. 

Over the years, she delved deeper into a world of substance abuse, including alcohol, marijuana, ecstasy, acid, cocaine, heroin, and methamphetamine.

'My addiction began after experiencing a series of traumas during my childhood,' she said. 'I was sexually abused, physically abused, lost a child, and then the father of that child died.

'My addiction began after experiencing a series of traumas during my childhood,' she said. 'I was sexually abused, physically abused, lost a child, and then the father of that child died'

'Drugs and alcohol helped numb the shame, guilt, pain, and grief I felt. My addiction also gave me a sense of belonging. I thought I had friends in the group of users I hung out with.'

In her late teens, cocaine became Sonya's drug of choice, leading her to engage in destructive behaviors such as lying, stealing, and self-harm. 

Her life spiraled downward as she fell into an abusive relationship, faced financial ruin, and turned to cheaper but equally destructive drugs like methamphetamine and opioids to satisfy her addiction.

'I found that while methamphetamine wasn't as intense as the high cocaine gave me, it lasted a lot longer and was a lot cheaper,' she explained. 'I was using methamphetamine in conjunction with opioids, consuming pills like Lortabs and OxyContin daily.' 

Sonya's parents made desperate attempts to rescue their daughter by providing her with money, a car, and a place to stay, but she would squander the money on drugs, leading to eviction and homelessness. 

Her addiction also cost her driver's license, resulting in multiple traffic violations and DUI arrests.

Eventually, Johnson managed to break free from her abusive boyfriend. She relocated from Florida to Georgia, where she met her now-husband and father of her two children, Steven.

'My husband was also struggling with drug addiction,' Sonya noted, 'But for a few months, we tried to live drug-free. 

Sonya said she swapped cocaine for methamphetamine because it 'lasted a lot longer and was a lot cheaper' 

When Sonya relocated from Florida to Georgia, she met her now-husband and father of her two children, Steven, who was also struggling with addiction 

Sonya said that when she got pregnant, she made the decision to stay clean. She and Steven married and then welcomed a second child 

'When I became pregnant, I made the decision to stay clean for the sake of my unborn child. After giving birth, my husband and I married and welcomed another child into our family.'

However, the couple soon found themselves ensnared by drugs once again, with Sonya resorting to intravenous drug use. 

The consequences of their addiction escalated when the Department of Children and Family Services arrived at their home and administered a drug test.

'When I failed the test, they warned they would return in 45 days, promising to close the case if I passed a second test. When they returned, I passed by using synthetic urine,' Sonya recalled.

Just five days after the case closed, Sonya and her husband had a heated dispute over a stolen check, resulting in her jumping out of their car and sustaining an injury. A passerby called the police, and Steve was arrested for domestic violence.

To protect their children, Aiden, then four, and Nicholas, then six, from foster care, Sonya chose to have them live with their grandmother.

Two years later, Steven was diagnosed with chronic heart failure due to the extensive damage caused by drugs to his body. He spent six months in the hospital. 

By 2017, despite some improvement in his condition, Steven continued to use drugs when he was not incarcerated, resulting in repeated arrests and subsequent trips to rehab. 

This destructive cycle persisted for 18 months, causing a significant rift between Sonya and Steven.

'I'd lost my kids, and it felt like I'd lost my husband too I was so heartbroken. I went on a drug binge, spending weeks or months without contact with my kids. I engaged in risky behavior, sleeping with anyone who had drugs,' she said. 

'I kept going in and out of jail. There was also a dark and spiritually unsettling aspect to my life. The people I surrounded myself with were involved in dangerous activities like drug trafficking and witchcraft. 

'It was terrifying. I knew I didn't belong there but felt trapped and had nowhere else to turn.'

Sonya's turning point came on her birthday in July 2018 when she was arrested for driving without a license on her way to a drug house. 

Facing multiple felony charges, including possession of controlled substances and introducing contraband into a detention facility, she found herself in jail once again.

Sonya attended various meetings during her time in jail and was introduced to programs like Celebrate Recovery. 

A pivotal moment occurred in court when a woman who had experienced a similar journey advocated for Sonya to attend treatment instead of going to prison. This intervention marked a turning point, and Sonya was given a chance at recovery.

In July 2019, Sonya achieved sobriety, and, in the following year, regained custody of her children. Rebuilding their relationship was not easy, as her children had endured years of absence and pain. Still, Sonya worked diligently to earn their trust and love back.

'After being absent from their lives for so long, my children initially found it hard to trust me, offer hugs, or express love. They were understandably angry, and I had to take responsibility for my past actions,' she said.

'I allowed them to feel their emotions and gradually showed them, one day at a time, that I could be the mother they deserved. Now, at ages 13 and 15, we have a strong and healthy relationship,' she added.

Today, Sonya has been clean for five years, thanks to her involvement in a 12-step program. She and her husband, who also sought help and achieved sobriety, have rebuilt their relationship, focusing on self-improvement and supporting each other's recovery.

Sonya now works with the nonprofit National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI), assisting parents in navigating custody rights and making decisions in the best interest of their children through the Parents for Parents program.

'I'm focused on growing this program and helping more parents who, like me, have dealt with the challenges of the child welfare system, addiction, mental health issues, and incarceration,' she said. 

'Personally, I'm in therapy to work through my own traumas, and I'm still actively involved in my 12-step recovery program. I often visit rehabs and jails to share my own recovery journey.

'I cherish the time I spend with my family, especially my 13- and 15-year-old kids, who remind me of the importance of living in the moment and making the most of it. My main life goal is simply to embrace every moment,' she added. 

'Right now, my husband and I are in a season of growth - he's started his own business, and we're both approaching five years of sobriety. Life is looking up, and I'm enjoying every moment, excited to see where this journey takes me next.'

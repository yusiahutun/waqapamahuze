Growth rings on 500-year-old clams reveal 'worrying' evidence of climate change

The oldest living animals in the world, quahog clams, have revealed 'worrying' data about man-made climate change.

Scientists studied the growth rings on the shells of clams and were able to map out the oceans temperature over the past 1,000 years.

Normally, changes in the Earth's climate and atmosphere are driven by natural changes in the ocean.

But the study claims to reveal that this pattern has flipped since the industrial revolution, when humans started emitting large amounts of greenhouse gases.

Quahog clams (pictured) are the world's longest living animals. They can live for more than 500 years, and the oldest one ever found, 507-year-old Ming, was killed by researchers who tried to find out how old it was

Professor Ian Hall of Cardiff University, who co-led the study, told the Independent that: 'The atmosphere is warming much quicker and driving the ocean variability' and 'man-made climate variability is hugely worrying.'

Quahog clams lay down annual growth rings on their shells, which help scientists date them.

The researchers collected 21 live and dead clam shells from the North Iceland Shelf, 263 feet (80 metres) deep into the ocean. 

The clams were dated using radiocarbon dating and by counting the rings on their shells.

Chemical components of the shells, known as isotopes, were measured to determine the ocean's temperature from AD 953–2000.

HOW DID THE STUDY WORK?

- The researchers collected 21 live and dead clam shells from the North Iceland Shelf, 263 feet (80 metres) deep into the ocean. 

- The clams were dated using radiocarbon dating and by counting the rings on their shells.

- Chemical components of the shells, known as isotopes, were measured to determine the ocean's temperature from AD 953–2000.

The findings, which were published in the journal Nature Communications, indicate that before the Industrial revolution, the North Atlantic Ocean played a key role in regulating the Northern Hemisphere's air temperature. 

Normally, changes in Earth's oceans are driven by radiation from the sun and volcanic eruptions.

These natural changes in the ocean influence the Earth's atmosphere. 

However, since 1800, at around the time the industrial revolution began, changes in the atmosphere have been taking place before or at the same time as changes in the ocean, instead of after. 

The results hint that increasing greenhouse gas emissions may indeed contribute to climate change.

Quahog clams develop annual growth rings which allow researchers to date them, just like the rings inside tree trunks

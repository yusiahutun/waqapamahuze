The 17 Best Hyaluronic Acid Serums, Tested and Reviewed

There may not be a more popular — or more controversial — skincare ingredient than hyaluronic acid. It’s kind of the green juice of skincare: It’s everywhere, no one’s really clear on what it does, and everyone wants it. In a nutshell, this sugar molecule is found naturally within our bodies alongside collagen and elastin, all of which start to slow down as we age, allowing dryness, dullness, and fine lines to creep in.

But here's the good news: Hyaluronic acid is humectant, and when it is applied to skin topically in the form of a serum or moisturizer, it becomes a moisture magnet, attracting water from the air and guzzling it down. This new surge of water content on the skin's surface can help a complexion look more youthful, with plumped up fine lines, and an all-over hydrated, fresh-faced vibrance.

To weed out the ineffective wannabes, we spent two months and over 400 hours testing the buzziest HA-based serums out there on our own skin. With each application, we scrutinized our skin for increased levels of hydration, softer and less noticeable fine lines, and smoother, bump-free texture. We also honed in on the experience overall, especially the way each serum felt on our skin throughout the day, noting any lingering stickiness, residue, or heaviness, along with other potential annoyances, like scent, absorption speed, and lackluster packaging. After we collected and evaluated the data from our editors, we scored and ranked each hyaluronic acid serum to curate this list, only bringing you the ones that delivered the very best results.

What to Keep in Mind

Formula

The good news is that most ingredients work very well when paired with hyaluronic acid. However, “the exceptions are other acids with low pHs, such as glycolic acid, which may degrade the HA and render it useless,” says board-certified dermatologist Dr. Rachel Nazarian. Instead, she suggests seeking formulas that use ingredients such as vitamin C to protect against free radicals or retinol to target wrinkles and improve skin elasticity.

If you have super dry skin, Dr. Nazarian recommends a hyaluronic acid serum with emollients that will hydrate and soften the skin, like glycerine, squalene, and jojoba oil. Due to its formula that contains plenty of these types of ingredients, we found the Dermalogica Circular Hydration Serum with Hyaluronic Acid did an exceptional job at moisturizing our flaky, cracked complexions.

Percentage of Hyaluronic Acid

“Hyaluronic acid serums with concentrations less than 2% are recommended, as anything above this can actually dry out the skin,” says board-certified plastic and reconstructive surgeon, Dr. Umbareen Mahmood.

Since hyaluronic acid on its own doesn’t absorb into the skin very well, you may also pay attention to the molecular weight of the hyaluronic acid in the formula you use. For example, Dr. Nazarian explains that “the type of HA we often like to use is sodium hyaluronate, because the molecular weight is lower, allowing it to penetrate more deeply into the skin.”

Skin Type

Our experts agree that all skin types can not only tolerate hyaluronic acid, but each one will benefit from it in some way. “I like suggesting hyaluronic acid for people that are on a skin care regimen that may be somewhat irritating,” says Dr. Nazarian, who adds that the HA “tends to offset some of the irritation that is found with acne ingredients, or retinoids.”

Your Questions, Answered

What is hyaluronic acid?

Technically speaking, hyaluronic acid is a chain of sugars known as disaccharides found in the body's connective tissue, says board-certified dermatologist Dr. Mona Gohara, but from a surface level HA is a “little molecule that helps to absorb water into the skin — it’s a powerful, strong humectant that occurs naturally in our skin,” she explains. She tells us to think of it like a miniature sponge that absorbs water, drawing it into the skin and holding it there to give your complexion a plump, firm appearance.

This makes it a fantastic option for anyone with superficial wrinkles or fine lines and those experiencing skin sagging, says Dr. Nazarian, “because it can minimize those lines very nicely, even within a few hours.”

Though these effects may be temporary, as we age our bodies produce less hyaluronic acid, so truly anyone can get a headstart at keeping it replenished by incorporating this acid into their rotation.

What's the best way to use hyaluronic acid serums?

Since hyaluronic acid is a humectant that draws water into the skin, Dr. Mahmood recommends applying it to damp skin after cleansing so that the acid acts like a magnet, attracting all of the moisture on the skin, enabling it to retain the hydration for a longer period of time.

The damp surface causes the acid to bind to the extra moisture and allows the formula to help your skin retain the hydration longer, and topping the serum off with a face lotion to further seal in moisture. “It can be used every day with no issue and can be used as part of your morning or evening skincare routine,” she adds.

If you’re seeking a more structured routine, Dr. Gohara suggests cleansing in the morning, followed by serum that contains an antioxidant like vitamin C, then using your hyaluronic acid before applying a moisturizer and sunscreen. At night, you can follow similar steps by skipping the sunscreen and swapping the antioxidant step for retinol instead.

Can everyone use hyaluronic acid?

Since hyaluronic acid is a natural substance, Dr. Nazarian says it’s well tolerated by nearly everyone. “There’s no skin type that can't use it because it's a naturally occurring substance that’s a hallmark of healthy skin,” she says.

Dr. Mahmood also explains that “despite the word 'acid' in the name, products containing HA are safe for sensitive skin and have not been found to have any known side effects.” She adds that it’s even one of the few skincare ingredients considered safe for a person who is pregnant or breastfeeding.

One outlier, says Dr. Gohara, is anyone with “inflammatory skin conditions such as acne or eczema,” who should always consult a dermatologist before using hyaluronic acid.

UK Pet Laws 4 Dog Breeds Banned In The UK

It's important for all UK pet owners to be aware of the various UK pet laws put in place by the government to ensure the welfare of both animals and owners.

A 2018 study revealed that the pet population in the UK was an estimated 51 million, which means that around 45% of British households own a pet.

The legal experts at the University of Law have highlighted the legislations that pet owners must take note of – from how your dog behaves in public to travelling with your pets, consider the laws below to ensure the safety of your pet.

4 most common pet laws in the UK

1. Pets on their travels

The government has implemented a variety of legislations to cover the welfare of pets and to prevent animal cruelty, including the Animal Welfare Act in 2006. According to the act, all pet owners must ensure that animals are safely secured when travelling in a car in the back. Any owners who allow their pet to sit in the front could face a fine up to £5,000.

Ensuring your pet is correctly secured in a car will also keep your animal safe on the road and avoid any unnecessary accidents.

2. Pets and licenses

When adopting a dog, it's important owners check that the breed isn't banned in the UK. If you're found to have a dog that is banned, you can be taken to court and face an unlimited fine or even be sent to prison for up to six months.

Some of the banned dog breeds include:

❌ Japanese Tosa

❌ Pit Bull Terrier

❌ Dogo Argentino

❌ Fila Braziliero

Read more about dogs banned in the UK here.

Licenses are also required for other exotic types of animals, too. Some types of snakes, spiders and domestic-wild hybrid animals (such as hybrids cats and wolfdogs) may also need licenses, so be sure to research throughly before you adopt any. The pet immediately becomes your responsibility once you have taken them home, so ensure you check the breed first to avoid difficulties after.

3. Dogs must be microchipped

All dogs must be microchipped by the time they are eight-weeks-old. Owners can face a £500 fine if this is not done. Microchipping can be done by your vet, but there are a number of organisations that can do it for free, including Battersea Dogs and Cats Home, the Blue Cross and Dogs Trust.

The rules vary slightly for Scotland and Wales. Read about them here.

4. Pets behaving badly

As an owner, you are responsible for the behaviour of your pet in public places. If your dog causes damage to other people's property or your cat digs up a neighbour's flowerbed, it is your duty as the owner to be held accountable. Keeping your dog on a lead when walking them will ensure they are kept under control.

For everything you need to know about pet laws in the UK, visit the government advice pages.

Some more obscure UK pet laws

• Daventry Council in Northamptonshire may give dog-walkers found to not be carrying a waste bag up to a £100 fine

• In Lancashire, it’s against the law to make a dog bark, unless instructed to do so by the police

• Up until 1965, allowing your dog to mate with a dog from the Royal House was an executable offence. It’s not so strict anymore, but still against the law so it is advised to respect it

• If you’re planning on walking your cows along the road between 10pm and 7am, think again. This is against the law according to Metropolitan Streets Act of 1867.

• Speaking of farm animals, it’s also illegal to have a pig sty at the front of your house according to the Town Police Clauses Act 1847.

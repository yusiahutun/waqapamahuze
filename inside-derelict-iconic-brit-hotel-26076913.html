Inside derelict iconic Brit hotel with hanging chandeliers and eerie cartoons on walls

The inside of a now derelict hotel, once visited by legendary author Lewis Carroll and naturalist Charles Darwin, has been revealed for the first time since a devastating fire last year.

The Ocean Hotel in Sandown was built in 1899, and sold to a commercial property developer last year. It had become known as somewhat of an eyesore in the local area, due to its shear size and derelict condition.

Sadly, the historic site suffered from a huge blaze, in a suspected act of arson, just a few weeks later, and is now at risk of being knocked down altogether.

Local news reports state that the 97-bedroom hotel had become home to squatters, but they were all cleared out after the sale. The hotel had previously housed a heated swimming pool and landscaped gardens.

It was designed by West End magnate Henry Lowenfeld, and was built on top of the former King's Head hotel. It contained a staircase belonging to the original hotel, which is now in poor condition.

UK Explorer, an Urban Explorer on Facebook, visited the site this week and found that there appears to be little left of the original building worth saving.

The grand rooms and hotel spaces have been stripped back to virtually nothing, with water damage and vandalism can be seen throughout - and one room even has random Disney cartoons on the walls.

One room, for some reason, houses thousands of lumps of black coal, while the historic name façade is clinging on for dear life.

When the sale took place last year, Councillor Ian Ward was positive about the site's future.

But the damage caused by the fire just a few weeks later, as evidenced by the photos of blackened roof beams and collapsed ceilings, would be any kind of hope of a future for the site very slim.

He said, shortly after the fire: “The positive is that it was already sold, which was the biggest hurdle. The fire still makes the building recoverable and it will be brought back into life.

“Looking at further positives for the town, the Pier Street toilets are going ahead and a couple of other derelict buildings have now got planning permission to be rebuilt.

“There is a lot going on behind the scenes and the Dinosaur Isle project will put £20million-plus into the town. It’s not all doom and gloom.”

Facebook users expressed their dismay at the state of such a historic building.

Wendy Hayden wrote: “Was such a pity that after the activist protectors moved out, having been given assurances that security would keep it safe, that the fire broke out. Make of that what you will.

“I'm pretty sure I remember reading it was the part with most history that got badly damaged.”

And Julia Courtney added: “My grandfather worked there in 1903, I have his work reference. It must have been lovely, so sad to see it now.”

She posted: “My late husband and I spent our first holiday on our own there after the family were off our hands. A holiday I remember with fondness in 1986 - so sad.”

The exact closure date of the hotel is not known, as it changed hands several times over the last few years of its life, but it was thought to stop functioning as a hotel in the mid 2000s.

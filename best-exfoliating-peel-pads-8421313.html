What to Keep in Mind

Resurfacing Ingredients

The first thing you’ll notice when shopping for chemical peels is how many ingredient options there are to choose from. The most common ingredients you’ll find, however, are alpha-hydroxy acids (AHAs) and beta-hydroxy acids (BHAs). “AHAs have a larger molecule than BHAs and help to remove dead skin off the surface of the stratum corneum,” explains Dr. Mian.

According to Lisa Chevalier, M.M.S., PA-C and founder of SoVous Medical Spa and Laser Center, they help to gently disrupt the bonds between the cells in the upper layers of the skin. “As we age, our cells turn over more slowly due to cellular senescence, and a little help from a peel pad can be just the thing to keep that youthful glow and aid in that turnover that makes young skin so smooth, bright and hydrated,” she says.

Unquestionably, AHAs glycolic and lactic acids are the most popular ingredients found in chemical peels. “They can help brighten the skin's dull complexion, lighten pigmentation, and smooth out skin texture,” says Dr. Mian. You’ll find both in Dr. Dennis Gross Alpha Beta Universal Daily Peel Pads. They’re not the only AHAs out there, though. Chevalier also recommends AHAs mandelic acid, malic acid, tartaric acid, and citric acid, which is often used to adjust the pH of a skincare product while doubling as an antioxidant. You can find lactic, glycolic, and citric acids in BABOR Refine RX AHA Peeling Pads. 

BHAs feature a smaller molecule that is oil soluble. “It penetrates deeply into the pores, loosening and removing pore clogging dirt, debris, and skin cells,” explains Dr. Sobel. Chevalier adds that it offers antibacterial and antiseptic properties, making salicylic acid an all-star ingredient for treating acne. You’ll find it in Jan Marini Marini Clear Multi-Acid Corrective Pads.

After AHAs and BHAs, there are PHAs, aka polyhydroxy acids. “These are buzzy new acids that are gentle enough for sensitive skin use,” says Dr. Sobel. In fact, Chevalier says that they’re so gentle that they won’t increase sun sensitivity like most exfoliants and can even be used on clients that have rosacea and eczema. They won’t penetrate as deeply as other acids, but they still can help smooth and brighten the skin. StriVectin Daily Reveal ™ Exfoliating Face Pads with AHA + BHA + PHA + TXA features PHA gluconolactone to add an exfoliation boost without increasing the risk of irritation. 

For those who like “clean” options for exfoliation, consider fruit enzymes. “Fruit enzymes like papaya, pineapple, and grapefruit are excellent exfoliators gentle enough for sensitive skin,” says Dr. Mian. They work similarly to the digestive enzymes in your gut to break down protein, except in this case, it’s breaking down the proteins securing dead skin cells to the surface, gently sweeping them away. You’ll find papaya enzymes in ELF SuperGlow AHA Peel Pads.

Other common exfoliators include retinoids, which work more deeply within the skin to promote cellular turnover and can thus help with deeper discoloration and skin laxity, as well as azelaic acid. Though you might think this ingredient is an AHA or BHA, it’s not, though they offer similar benefits. While AHAs and BHAs are derived from ingredients like sugarcane (glycolic acid), milk (lactic acid), and an aspirin precursor (salicylic acid), azelaic acid is a byproduct of yeast. In addition to exfoliating skin, it can help balance the microbiome and reduce inflammation. 

Your Skin Concerns + Sensitivity

With all the chemical exfoliant options out there, it can be a challenge to determine which is right for your skincare needs. When in doubt, start with glycolic acid. “Glycolic acid is a superhero of acids because it works on most skin types, from breakout-prone to mature skin,” says Dr. Sobel. “It is very effective at treating many concerns including acne, scarring, sun damage, and dark spots and hyperpigmentation.” It’ll also make your skin appear more radiant, leaving your skin glowing instantly after use. It also helps to brighten the skin for a more radiant, glowing complexion. It’s the main ingredient in Sonäge Glow to Go Glycolic Acid Peel Pads.

If you’re prone to skin sensitivity, you might want to reach for lactic acid (found in BeautyStat Triple Action One-Step Daily Exfoliating Peel Pad) or PHAs, which are gentler and work more slowly to exfoliate skin. “Fruit enzymes are also great for sensitive skin,” says Dr. Mian. You’ll find gentle exfoliants lactic acid and papaya enzymes in Lancer Skincare Gentle Exfoliating Peel Pads.

Those with oily, acne-prone, or rough skin, including those managing keratosis pilaris should try salicylic acid. “It gets inside pores to clear out the gunk that causes the appearance of enlarged pores and blackheads along the nose, forehead, and chin,” says Dr. Sobel. It’ll also help keep the openings of the hair follicles clear to help smooth out the coarse texture created by KP. This ingredient is in Skinfix Resurface+ AHA/BHA Niacinamide Exfoliating Pads for Face and Targeted Body, which can be used from the face down to keep skin smooth, clear, and bright.

“Most oily skin and mature skin can get away with using an all-in-one formulated peel pad,” says Chevalier. But don’t let skin sensitivity keep you from trying chemical peels. “Sensitive skin types should experiment with singular ingredients with products that take longer to penetrate,” she says. And beyond the ingredients you use, keep your usage in mind. “You don't have to use a chemical peel pad every day for it to be effective,” says Dr. Mian. “Those with sensitive skin can start using it once a week, and maybe work their way up to twice a week if the formula is gentle.”

Strength

Some peels will explicitly list the percentage of the active ingredients used in the formula, which will give you a sense of how powerful it is. For example, M-61 PowerGlow Peel Extra Strength 20% features 20 percent glycolic acid, which makes it pretty strong; TULA Breakout Breakthrough Acne Maximum Strength Biodegradable Toner Pads lists a 2 percent concentration of salicylic acid. You can also take a look at the ingredient list. “Ingredients are listed in descending order by concentration on the packaging, with the first ingredient having the highest and the last the lowest,” says Chevalier. SkinBetter Science AlphaRet Exfoliating Peel Pads features lactic acid as the second ingredient (after water), so you know that this is the most concentrated active in the formula.

Even if you’re not using a super potent peel, you’ll want to ease it into your regimen to give your skin time to adjust. Dr. Mian recommends using your chemical peel pad on a night when you’re not using a retinoid and starting your routine with a cream cleanser before going in with the peel. “Follow it with a hydrating serum, like hyaluronic acid, panthenol, or snail mucin,” she says, then “top it off with a moisturizer that has glycerin and ceramides.” This combination of ingredients will help keep your skin comfortable and nourished while helping to avoid too much irritation.

Reducing Irritation

Some sensitivity is par for the course when introducing chemical peel pads to your regimen. “They’re speeding up the process of cellular turnover, which will definitely sensitize the skin to the sun and other active ingredients,” explains Chevalier. Every time you’re using a peel pad, you’re removing a top layer of dead skin, which does offer some protection from external factors, so you’ll be leaving your skin with a weaker line of defense. “The act of the peel pad itself wiping on the skin can cause irritation,” adds Dr. Mian. “I prefer chemical peel pads that are smooth on the surface without ridges.”

Because peels can also dehydrate the skin, you’ll want to use milkier cleansers, which won’t be as stripping as a gel formula. “Use a high-quality, cleanly formulated hyaluronic serum to flood skin with weightless moisture and keep the skin barrier intact,” adds Dr. Sobel. You should also use a fatty acid- and ceramide-rich moisturizer, which will help protect your skin’s moisture barrier. Lastly, using a physical sunscreen is a must, says Chevalier.

Remember, you’ve exposed the fresh, sensitive skin underneath the dead skin to the elements, including UV — and considering so many peels are designed to treat sun damage, you don’t want to counteract all your hard work! A physical sunscreen will sit on the surface of the skin to act as a shield, blocking the sun’s rays without sensitizing the skin. “Chemical blockers are often irritating as they absorb into the skin that may already be sensitized,” says Chevalier.

Your Questions, Answered

What do exfoliating peel pads do?

Exfoliating peel pads do exactly what they claim — exfoliate! “They remove skin-dulling dead cells that can cause skin congestion and enlarged pores,” says Dr. Sobel. “They smooth uneven skin texture and can soften the appearance of fine lines and wrinkles.” Removing these dead cells can also improve the look of hyperpigmentation. 

As dead cells are removed from the skin’s surface, this process will stimulate your skin’s natural cellular turnover. This helps your skin look more radiant and fresh over time — but you’ll also see an instant glow after using an exfoliating peel pad.

When do I use exfoliating pads?

They’re best used at night because removing dead skin cells with chemical exfoliants can make your skin more sensitive to the sun (so be sure to use SPF in the AM!). Using your chemical peels at night will also be more effective, says Chevalier. “Your skin does a lot of resetting while you sleep allowing for better penetration of products,” she explains. “The temperature of the skin actually increases while you sleep and this causes blood vessels in the skin to dilate and aid in this product absorption.”

If you’re wondering how to integrate a chemical peel into a routine that already features a retinoid, Dr. Mian says she uses hers on the nights she’s not using her retinoid. This will help to prevent your skin from getting irritated.

Are exfoliating pads bad for your skin?

Not at all — they’re quite good for skin health when used correctly. “There are a slew of skin benefits to exfoliating peel pads when used appropriately and in conjunction with proper sun care,” says Chevalier. Adds Dr. Sobel, “they are very versatile, and can be used on the arms, back and other areas where KP or breakouts can occur.” However, if you’re overusing them or using a formula that’s not right for your skin type, that’s a different question. Overuse of chemical peels can result in over-exfoliation, which can leave your skin tender, inflamed, and irritated. If you get to this point, you’ll know it — even basic cleanser or moisturizer can make your skin feel like it’s burning. In this case, you may need to take a break from all skincare products, especially chemical peel pads, until your moisture barrier recovers. 

How often should you use exfoliating pads?

A lot of people ask if you can use exfoliating pads every day. The answer to that is “sometimes.” “If the formula is gentle and you have skin on the oily side, you may be able to tolerate a peel pad every day,” says Dr. Mian. In fact, using one with soothing ingredients daily may be beneficial to the skin. However, it’s really best to ease chemical peel pads into your routine, starting with once or twice weekly use. 

If you’re new to chemical peels, Chevalier recommends rinsing your skin after use, even if the formula doesn’t say to do so. “Many products can become too irritating if you're using other products that contain active ingredients,” she warns. As your skin acclimates, you can skip the neutralizing step (unless your formula is designed to be used with one!), see how your skin reacts to the peel, and then gradually increase your usage to four times a week. Again, some people may be able to use a peel every day;  some peels are formulated to be gentle enough to use them daily. Follow your skin’s lead!
